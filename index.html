Who It's For

Real people.
Real moments.

Unsaid is not a journaling app. It's not a chatbot. It's the first product built for the specific, urgent problem of emotional articulation — getting from what you feel to what you can say.

```
User Profile · Maya, 31 · Product Manager
"My dad has Alzheimer's. There's a window closing. I need to say something before he's gone."

She's tried to write it a dozen times. It comes out clinical or melodramatic. She can't find the version that sounds like her — not what she thinks she's supposed to say.

01
She Opens Unsaid

One question. "What haven't you been able to say?" No prompts, no templates. Just the question.

02
Unsaid Listens

She types fragments. Half-sentences. Unsaid doesn't rush to output. It asks three precision questions that go deeper, not broader.

"What's one thing he did that you never thanked him for — something small, that other people wouldn't even notice?"
03
Her Words, Found

Unsaid returns the letter she couldn't write. Not a template filled with her details — her voice, her memory, her emotional register. She reads it and starts crying.

04
She Releases It

She sends the letter to her father. She also releases an anonymous version into the Unsaid stream — where a stranger in Seoul reads it and feels less alone.

User Profile · James, 27 · Software Engineer
"I said something awful to my best friend two years ago. I've wanted to apologize ever since. I don't know how without making it about me."

Every apology he drafts sounds like he's asking for forgiveness, not offering it. He keeps rewriting. He never sends anything.

01
Two Years of Drafts

James opens Unsaid at midnight, types the whole story in 200 words. He's never written it out in one place before.

02
The Right Question

Unsaid asks: "What did you take from him when you said that?"

"Not what you did wrong — but what it cost him. That's where the real apology lives."
03
The Apology He Needed

The output is three short paragraphs. No dramatic declarations. Just clean accountability. He sends it in two minutes. His friend calls the next day.

User Profile · Priya, 34 · Teacher
"My partner and I have been together eight years. We don't say the big things anymore. I want to write her something for our anniversary but I sound like a Hallmark card."

The love is enormous. The language available to express it feels worn-out, borrowed from other people's stories.

01
Against the Cliché

02
Unsaid Goes Specific

"Tell me about a moment so small you almost didn't notice it — but you did."
```
